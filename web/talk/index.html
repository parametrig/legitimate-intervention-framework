Legitimate Overrides in Decentralized Protocols

Engineering emergency governance under time pressure

TERSE 2026 · Elem Oghenekaro · Dr. Nimrod Talmon

This talk is about emergency powers that already exist across protocols (pauses, freezes, forks). The central question is how to design mechanisms that are fast enough to work and constrained enough to remain legitimate.
01

The paradox

In crises, communities demand intervention. Outside crises, override capability reduces trust.

Even unused emergency powers impose a cost: they change the trust model. The point is to stop improvising and start specifying constraints ex ante.
02

Evidence

Dataset and scope

The argument is empirics-first. We map a design space against what actually happened. Heavy tails mean rare catastrophic events dominate. Design must optimize for tail risk, not averages.
03

Heavy tails

Super-hacks dominate the risk

This motivates emergency design: tails are where systems break. The question becomes: which mechanism minimizes total cost under tail risk?
04

Design space

Scope × Authority taxonomy

Reframe from "centralized vs decentralized" to mechanism design: what scope, triggered by whom, under what safeguards. Fast examples: - Asset scope + issuer authority: stablecoin blacklist/freeze. - Protocol scope + delegated authority: guardian pauses a protocol during exploit.
05

Objective

What are we optimizing?

Emergency mechanisms have a cost even when never used. We make that cost explicit and engineer against it. Bridge: Now we can treat legitimacy as an economic parameter, not a moral argument.
06

Model

Expected cost framing (intuition)

A protocol chooses architecture m ex ante. Events arrive with some distribution; losses accrue until contained. Audience anchor: this is like SRE incident response—you pay standing cost for reliability so you don't pay catastrophic cost later.
07

Empirics

Scope–Authority matching

Not simply "governance is slow." Governance tends to succeed where deliberation is the right tool (network recovery / history revision). Delegation can be the best-performing option for time-critical containment, but only with tight bounds.
08

Sentiment

Legitimacy cost is not fixed

Same mechanism can be perceived as protective or as discretion depending on community priors and process. This suggests culture-aware calibration rather than universal prescriptions.
09

Tooling

From debate to mechanism selection

Tooling converts framework into implementable selection. Designed to be critiqueable and adaptable by different communities. Demo option: show a mechanism comparison under two threat assumptions.
10

Principles

Actionable design takeaways

The point is a "constitution for emergencies" not ad hoc heroics. Minimum viable legitimacy checklist: - Pre-specified authority - Scope minimization - Evidence bundle - Time-bounded window - Ex post ratification + revert path
11

Anti-drift anchor

Layered stack: prevention, bounded response, tooling

This slide is the anti-drift anchor. It links prevention primitives to bounded response design, and both to the measurement/tooling loop.
12

Credible layers

Where they fit (and what they don't solve)

Prevention shifts the equilibrium, but does not remove the need for response design.
13

Comparison

Design schools in the market

Many products are easier to integrate because they sit at the dapp/RPC layer or scan mempool heuristically. Credible-layer style enforcement is heavier: you execute transactions during block production, take traces, and decide inclusion. The key advantage is exact-state validation: it's much harder to bypass with timing games.
