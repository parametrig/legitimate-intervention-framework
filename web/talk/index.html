Legitimate Overrides

Engineering emergency governance under time pressure

When $78.8B of exploits happen, “code is law” becomes a design problem.

Scope × Authority α ≈ 1.33 (heavy-tailed losses) Decision support tooling

TERSE 2026 · Elem Oghenekaro · Dr. Nimrod Talmon

This talk is about emergency powers that already exist across protocols (pauses, freezes, forks). The central question isn’t “intervene or not”—it’s how to design mechanisms that are fast enough to work and constrained enough to remain legitimate.
01

The paradox

In crises, communities demand intervention. Outside crises, override capability reduces trust.

Crisis mode

  • Users want containment
  • Integrators want continuity
  • Time-to-contain dominates

Normal mode

  • Override = standing backdoor
  • Legitimacy is contested
  • Even unused power has a cost
Even unused emergency powers impose a cost: they change the trust model. The point is to stop improvising and start specifying constraints ex ante.
02

Evidence

Dataset and scope

  • 705 documented exploit incidents (2016–2026)
  • Losses are heavy-tailed (power law), α ≈ 1.33
  • Intervention mechanisms appear across many ecosystems
The argument is empirics-first. We map a design space against what actually happened. Heavy tails mean rare catastrophic events dominate—design must optimize for tail risk, not averages.
03

Heavy tails

Super-hacks dominate the risk

  • ~80% of cumulative losses concentrated in a small number of incidents
  • Governance must handle rare, high-stakes events under time pressure
  • In heavy-tailed systems, one failure can dominate years of safe operation
This motivates emergency design: tails are where systems break. The question becomes: which mechanism minimizes total cost under tail risk?

05

Objective

What are we optimizing?

  • Containment speed (time-to-contain)
  • Blast radius (collateral disruption)
  • Standing centralization / legitimacy cost (“Chekhov’s gun”)

Most real decisions here aren’t risk vs safety. They’re risk vs risk: action risk vs inaction risk, where the baseline is not neutral.

Emergency mechanisms have a cost even when never used. We make that cost explicit and engineer against it. Bridge: Now we can treat legitimacy as an economic parameter, not a moral argument.

Model

Expected cost framing (intuition)

ExpectedCost(m) = CentralizationCost(m) + Σ Pr[h] · ( Time(m) · DamageRate(h) + BlastRate(m) )

  • Choose architecture m ex ante
  • Events arrive; losses accrue until contained
  • Standing cost trades off against speed and disruption
A protocol chooses architecture m ex ante. Events arrive with some distribution; losses accrue until contained. Audience anchor: this is like SRE incident response—you pay standing cost for reliability so you don’t pay catastrophic cost later.
07

Empirics

Scope–Authority matching

  • Signer sets dominate volume but show weaker success than governance where governance is used
  • Many failures are mis-allocations of authority to task
  • Delegation is often the practical sweet spot for time-critical containment (if bounded tightly)
Not simply “governance is slow.” Governance tends to succeed where deliberation is the right tool (network recovery / history revision). Delegation can be the practical sweet spot for time-critical containment, but bound it tightly.
08

Sentiment

Legitimacy cost is not fixed

  • Community sentiment modulates effective standing cost
  • Aggregate average sentiment: +0.028 (variance high)
  • Same mechanism can be seen as protection or discretion depending on priors + process
Same mechanism can be perceived as protective or as discretion depending on community priors and process. This suggests culture-aware calibration rather than universal prescriptions.

10

Principles

Actionable design takeaways

  • Prefer precision where possible (module/account vs protocol/network)
  • Use delegation with bounds for time-critical containment
  • Use governance where it’s strong: ratification, recovery, history revision
  • Make overrides bounded, auditable, time-limited, and reversible/ratifiable
The point is a “constitution for emergencies” not ad hoc heroics. Minimum viable legitimacy checklist: - Pre-specified authority - Scope minimization - Evidence bundle - Time-bounded window - Ex post ratification + revert path

13

Credible layers

Where they fit (and what they don’t solve)

  • Pre-execution assertion/invariant enforcement reduces discretionary response
  • Not a complete substitute: novel failures and cross-domain issues remain
  • Correct model: defense-in-depth, not a single mechanism
Prevention shifts the equilibrium, but does not remove the need for response design.
14

Comparison

Design schools in the market

API / RPC / mempool screening

  • Easier integration (API calls, heuristic screening)
  • Checks occur pre-block; exact state can be unknown
  • Can be bypassed via timing/state divergence

In-block enforcement (credible layer)

  • Validates at the exact state of execution (inside the block)
  • Harder to bypass under sophisticated timing games
  • Heavier integration: execution + trace during block production

Timing bypass (intuition)

t1: on the mempool → honest
t1: in the block after t2 → malicious

Many products are easier to integrate because they sit at the dapp/RPC layer or scan mempool heuristically. Credible-layer style enforcement is heavier: you execute transactions during block production, take traces, and decide inclusion. The key advantage is exact-state validation: it’s much harder to bypass with timing games.

Incentives

“Do nothing” is an equilibrium

  • Acting creates a signature
  • Inaction creates ambiguity
  • Ambiguity can be career-safe even when systemically harmful
This motivates why procedures matter: we need decision pathways that make correct action defensible.

Implementation

Patterns (how to embed legitimacy)

  • Optimistic freeze (fast action + governance ratification)
  • Pre-registered action registry (bounded authority)
  • Non-retroactivity for parameter changes
These are practical “constitutional” mechanisms.
